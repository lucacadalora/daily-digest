China's Steel Sector Seized by Talk of 'Supply Reform 2.0'

February 28, 2025 · 5 min read

China's billion-ton steel industry is edging toward its biggest shake-up in a decade, with speculation growing that Beijing will order plant closures in response to a construction slowdown at home and a wave of protectionism overseas.

The world's top steel-producing nation has hinted at a "Supply Reform 2.0" campaign that would mirror a previous effort that dramatically reduced capacity. The discussions have gained added impetus from China's drive to meet ambitious climate goals, but the underlying challenge is more fundamental – the country's vast metals sector faces an unprecedented drop in long-term demand.

China's real estate crisis has permanently diminished the need for steel in the construction sector, while export markets are increasingly closing due to tariffs and protectionism in the EU, US, and various Asian markets.

Citigroup is predicting Beijing may soon initiate a new round of production cuts totaling 50 million tons initially – equivalent to all of Germany's annual output – with more potentially to follow.

"The mills are concerned, and they should be," said Kevin Li, a senior ferrous consultant at CRU Group. "The industry has been in a state of oversupply for at least a couple of years."
